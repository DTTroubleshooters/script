## SLIDE 4 — The Core Problem
### 🎤 Speaker 2: Karthik *(continued)*

The problem is this: **contemporary lifestyle products are losing their essence, their identity, their authenticity, and their cultural roots.**

How? Look at the four forces at play.

**Globalization** — mass production has started erasing regional craft identities. A mekhela chador you find in a mall today may look like Muga silk, but it has no story, no geography, no face behind it.

**Commercialization** — traditional patterns are being stripped of their spiritual and cultural meaning. They become "prints." They become "motifs." They become wallpaper.

**Design Homogeneity** — global aesthetics are replacing local visual languages. A buyer in Tokyo and a buyer in Guwahati are sometimes being sold the same product, rebranded.

**Knowledge Erosion** — artisan techniques are not being passed to the next generation. When the master weaver retires, decades of embodied knowledge disappear with them.

But here's the thing — this is *also* a design opportunity. Technology can reconnect heritage with modern life. CAD tools, AI pattern generation, e-commerce platforms, and authentication technology can breathe new life into ancient crafts — *if used thoughtfully.*

That brings us to why we chose Muga silk as our focus. Over to Akash.

## SLIDE 6 — Muga Silk: Assam's Golden Thread
### 🎤 Speaker 3: Karthik *(continued)*

Let me give you some numbers.

Muga silk is produced exclusively from the *Antheraea assamensis* silkworm — found only in the Brahmaputra valley. There is literally nowhere else on earth where this silk comes from.

Assam produces **95% of India's total Muga output.** The industry supports **3.19 lakh families** across **9,935 sericulture villages.** And the price of Muga yarn has risen from ₹800 per kilogram in the 1990s to **₹21,000 per kilogram today** — a signal of both the premium potential of this craft and the pressure on buyers to find cheaper alternatives.

Culturally, Muga silk is worn at Bihu festivals, weddings, and religious ceremonies. It is passed down as an heirloom. It *is* Assamese identity in textile form.

And yet — it faces three simultaneous existential threats.

**Climate.** Silkworms are extremely sensitive to heat and rainfall variations. Erratic monsoons and rising temperatures directly disrupt rearing cycles.

**Counterfeiting.** Original Muga designs are being copied onto cheap synthetic fabric. A GI tag was registered in 2006 — but enforcement is nearly zero. Fake products are destroying both income and reputation for genuine weavers.

**Youth Migration.** No artisan we spoke to had children willing to continue weaving. City service jobs offer shorter hours and a predictable monthly income. The loom cannot compete — yet.

This is why we chose Muga silk. The problem is real, urgent, and solvable. Apratim will now take you into the research we conducted.

## SLIDE 8 — Field Interview: Voices of the Artisans
### 🎤 Speaker 4: Apratim *(continued)*

We conducted primary interviews with Muga silk weavers in Upper Assam — translated from Assamese.

Let me share one quote that captures everything:

*"Designs made on genuine Muga silk get copied and woven on cheap material. Gullible customers get cheated. They don't have the patience to sit and weave for long hours."*
— **Shri Jayanta Nath, Muga Silk Weaver, Upper Assam**

This one sentence holds three crises in it — counterfeiting, consumer unawareness, and the unsustainability of the craft as a livelihood.

Breaking it down into the three challenges our artisans named:

**Challenge #1 — Climate Change.** Silkworms are highly sensitive to heat and rain. Erratic weather disrupts rearing cycles and cocoon drying. In the rainy season, weavers have to use firewood ovens to dry cocoons — adding cost and carbon.

**Challenge #2 — Market Counterfeiting.** Fake designs are sold cheaply. The GI tag, which was registered in 2006, is effectively unenforced. Less than 15% of buyers verify authenticity before purchasing. Genuine weavers lose both income and the reputation that took generations to build.

**Challenge #3 — Youth Migration.** No artisan's children are willing to continue the craft. Peak season work runs from 8 AM to 9 PM. That is a 13-hour day, with no weekends, no sick leave, and unpredictable seasonal income. Urban service jobs are simply more appealing on paper.

## SLIDE 9 — Design Thinking Survey Report
### 🎤 Speaker 4: Apratim *(continued)*

We also ran a consumer survey to map the demand side of this crisis — using the empathy framework of Says, Does, Thinks, and Feels.

**What people SAY:** About 40% say weaving is a "great side hustle, but not a main career." Many actively advise youth to look for city jobs instead. On price, people are split — ₹21,000 per kilogram is seen as premium by some, but overpriced compared to synthetics by others.

**What people DO:** More than half of our respondents didn't even know there is an official GI tag for Muga silk. Most have zero direct exposure to the handloom process. And less than 15% check for an authenticity mark every time they buy traditional attire.

**What people THINK:** Market fraud and youth migration are widely recognized as the top threats. There's a strong belief that automated loom technology is critical for the industry's survival.

**How people FEEL:** When they hear about counterfeiting, people feel sadness — they see it as a systemic failure — or protective anger, viewing it as theft of cultural identity. Watching a weaver work a 13-hour day triggers deep respect, but also concern. A smaller segment feels completely disconnected — the artisan's world feels too far removed from their own lives.

This emotional disconnect is itself a design problem. And that's where Suhrudh takes over — with the empathy tools we used to go deeper.

## SLIDE 11 — Empathy Map & User Persona
### 🎤 Speaker 5: Ashish *(continued)*

We built our empathy tools around a single, representative user: **Shri Jayanta Nath**, a Muga silk weaver from Assam, 55 to 60 years old, who has been weaving since childhood.

His archetype quote: *"I have been doing this work since childhood and want to continue it while creating new designs."* — That line carries both pride and an undercurrent of uncertainty.

From the **Empathy Map**, we captured four dimensions of his experience:

He **Says** things like: "Climate change is the biggest challenge for us." "Fake products are harming our work and reputation." "My children are not interested in continuing this work."

He **Thinks**: The future of this craft is uncertain. The market is unfair. His skills are valuable but may not be preserved.

He **Does**: Starts work at 8 AM, continues until 9 PM in peak season. Converts cocoons into silk thread by hand. Designs patterns and colour combinations entirely on his own. Adapts his technique based on weather conditions.

He **Feels**: Proud of his craftsmanship. Frustrated by fakes. Worried about climate. Disappointed that his children won't carry the craft forward. Emotionally connected to tradition. Anxious about the future.

The key **contradictions** we found: He says the work is valuable — but the next generation is not continuing it. He wants to preserve tradition — but works completely alone without successors. He believes his skill is unique — but the market does not reward it fairly.

## SLIDE 12 — Artisan Journey Map
### 🎤 Speaker 5: Akash *(continued)*

The Journey Map plots his emotional experience across five stages of production:

**Cocoon Preparation** → **Thread & Design** → **Weaving** → **Market & Sale** → **Legacy & Future**

At the start, emotion is slightly positive — around 2 out of 10 — because the process is anxious. Unpredictable weather, the need for perfect drying conditions.

It rises to a 6 during Thread & Design — this is where skill and creativity come in. He feels purposeful.

Weaving brings it to a 4 — deep concentration, physical strain, satisfaction when the weave is precise.

Market & Sale crashes to minus 6 — the deep frustration of counterfeits, weak GI enforcement, and isolation from the market.

And Legacy & Future sits at minus 3 — resigned hope. He wants to continue. He wants to develop new designs. But none of his children will sit at the loom after him.

The lowest point on that experience curve — the market stage — is exactly where our solution is designed to intervene.

## SLIDE 13 — Root Cause Analysis (5 Whys)
### 🎤 Speaker 5: Suhrudh *(continued)*

To understand the root causes, we used the 5 Whys framework.

The **central problem** we identified is low and unstable income for artisans — driven primarily by middlemen taking high margins.

But why is that happening?

**Environmental:** Climate change disrupts silkworm rearing and cocoon drying — cutting yields unpredictably.

**Market Flooding:** Counterfeits depress prices. The GI tag exists but has near-zero enforcement. There is no accessible authentication system for buyers.

**Economic Instability:** Artisans have no direct market access. High production costs. They are dependent on local traders who set prices.

**Generational:** Youth migrate. No financial incentive to stay. Low awareness of the craft's cultural and economic value.

**Working Conditions:** 13-hour days. Physically demanding work. No modern equipment. No formal training.

Each of these is a separate design challenge — and together they form a system that is slowly grinding down an irreplaceable heritage. Soham will now walk you through how we tackled ideation.

## SLIDE 15–16 — How Might We...? (HMW Questions)
### 🎤 Speaker 6: Praneeth *(continued)*

We generated **fifteen "How Might We" questions** covering every dimension of the crisis.

From the rearing side: *How might we keep silkworms alive in extreme heat? How might we reduce physical labor in rearing?*

From the production side: *How might we make the handloom more comfortable to sit at? How might we help weavers finish a saree in half the time? How might we reduce eye strain caused by fine weaving?*

From the market side: *How might we prove silk is 100% real? How might we help customers understand the high price? How might we find new products beyond sarees?*

From the technology side: *How might we use mobile apps to help rural weavers? How might we use machines without losing the handmade feel?*

And from the bigger picture: *How might we make weaving a dream job for teenagers? How might we ensure weavers get paid instantly for their work?*

We then ran a **6-3-5 brainwriting session** — six participants, three ideas each, five rounds — generating over ninety ideas across all fifteen questions. The room was full of sketches, scrawled notes, and some genuinely inventive thinking: passive cooling sheds, digital jacquard looms, QR tag authentication, WhatsApp-based order management, weaver storytelling apps, and more.

## SLIDE 18 — Section Break: Our Solution
### 🎤 Speaker 7: Karthik

Thank you, Soham.

Every problem we identified — every tear in the map, every low point on the journey curve, every root cause in the 5 Whys — pointed to one fundamental gap: **there is no way for a buyer to verify that what they're holding is real, and no way for the artisan to receive fair value for what they've made.**

The Craft Passport is designed to close that gap.

---

## SLIDE 19 — Craft Passport
### 🎤 Speaker 7: Suhrudh *(continued)*

Here is how it works — in four steps.

**Step 1 — Artisan Updates.** The weaver sends a WhatsApp voice message in Assamese: "Started weaving batch number 47." No smartphone app to install. No training required. Just a voice note — the same way they might message a family member.

**Step 2 — AI Transcribes.** An AI system processes the message, transcribes it, and digitises the production update. The information is recorded on a blockchain — tamper-proof, time-stamped, permanent.

**Step 3 — Unique QR Generated.** A unique QR code is generated and physically stitched onto the product. This code is the product's identity document — its passport.

**Step 4 — Buyer Scans.** The buyer scans the QR code and sees the complete artisan journey — who made it, where, when, which stage of production, the GI certification status, and the artisan's own story.

The security features: **Blockchain Verification** — tamper-proof, cannot be forged. **GI Registry Integration** — authenticated against the official Geographical Indication database. **End-to-End Traceability** — from harvesting, rearing, dyeing, and weaving, all the way to the sale and GI certification.

And critically — **no middlemen.** Artisans sell directly, receive payments instantly, and the price manipulation by local traders is eliminated.

---


## SLIDE 21 — Expected Impact
### 🎤 Speaker 7: everyone *(continued)*

Let me close with what the Craft Passport actually *changes.*

**Zero Fakes.** Every product carries a tamper-proof blockchain record. Counterfeiting becomes technologically impossible at scale. One scan, and the buyer knows.

**Fair Pay.** Direct artisan-to-buyer sales with instant digital payments. No price manipulation. The weaver receives what their work is actually worth.

**Global Reach.** The QR code doesn't just authenticate — it tells a story. Global luxury and sustainable fashion markets are growing at 8 to 10% CAGR. The Craft Passport connects Assamese weavers to that demand, directly.

**GI Enforced.** The 2006 GI tag finally has real teeth. Integration with the GI Registry gives artisans an accessible complaint mechanism. Online counterfeiting — which has been essentially unpunishable — now leaves a traceable record.

**Youth Pull.** This is perhaps the most important impact. Weavers become digital storytellers. Their profiles build followings. The craft becomes legible, modern, and verifiable to the next generation. The "dream job" potential is restored.

**Cultural Continuity.** The full traceability chain — Harvesting, Rearing, Dyeing, Weaving, Sale, Certification — is a living record of the craft. That chain is heritage. That chain is what we are preserving.
